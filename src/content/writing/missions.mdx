In my third-period pull-out, I have a sixth grader who won't start a Mission until I sit next to her. Not because she can't read it. Because the first screen shows six things at once, and she's decided the whole thing is too much before she's read a word.

Everything I designed after that started with the same question: *what's the first thing she sees?*

## The rename

I stopped calling them lessons in October. Not because "lessons" is wrong — it's the accurate word — but because it's the word she'd heard eight thousand times before she landed in my room, and every one of those times, it had preceded a page of things she couldn't do.

A mission is different. A mission has a briefing, an objective, a target. A mission can be *launched*. A mission ends with a stamp on the file that says the mission is complete.

The word is small. The reframe is not.

## What actually changed

**One idea per screen.** The old lesson deck had a slide with a definition, an example, a non-example, a question, and a "your turn" prompt. All at once. The new mission has five screens, and each one has one of those things on it. Same content, same time budget, radically different affect in the room.

**A named narrator.** The Support Commander shows up in the top-left corner of every screen. She names the student. She names the objective. She never lectures. She asks one question at a time and waits.

**A visible progress bar.** Not a percentage. A path — twelve nodes, one per mission, and you can see where you are on it. Kids who are three missions in *know* they are three missions in. That knowledge is worth more than any of the vocabulary I taught them.
